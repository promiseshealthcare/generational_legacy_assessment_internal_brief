Internal product brief — Promises Healthcare

Generational Legacy Assessment

A structured documentation programme for UHNW patients — conducted by Dr Joseph Leong, Senior Consultant Psychiatrist

What this is

The Generational Legacy Assessment is a three-part structured documentation programme for UHNW individuals who wish to ensure their legacy intentions are recorded with the rigour necessary to withstand scrutiny. It is not a clinical or psychiatric assessment programme. Its purpose is to create a contemporaneous, professionally witnessed record of the patient's clarity of mind, intentions, and family context — produced at a specific point in time, signed by a Senior Consultant Psychiatrist, and suitable for reference in trust, estate, and capacity proceedings.

How to position this to patients

When the patient says: "I cannot change my family's mindset"

"You don't need to. What we are doing is not about changing anyone's mind. It is about making sure that your mind — your clarity, your intentions, your wishes at this moment — is so thoroughly documented that it speaks for itself long after this conversation. Your family's mindset becomes irrelevant when the record is this strong."

The core reframe

This is not a family therapy exercise. This is a legacy protection measure. The patient is not being asked to manage their family. They are being given the means to ensure that what they have built cannot be dismantled after they are gone. Frame it as an act of agency, not an admission of vulnerability.

Pricing justification

The fee is not for clinical time. It is for a signed, professionally produced document that may determine the outcome of proceedings involving assets of significant value. UHNW patients routinely spend multiples of this figure on estate planning and legal fees. The Generational Legacy Assessment belongs in the same category — not in the same conversation as a therapy session.

Referral pathway

The most effective referral channel is the patient's own lawyer — specifically private wealth and trust partners who are already advising on estate and succession matters. When a trusted partner says "I would strongly recommend Dr Joseph's assessment before we finalise this," the patient does not need to be independently convinced. The 17 June roundtable and the pre-meeting 1:1 conversations with partners are the primary mechanism for seeding this referral behaviour.
