Generational Legacy Assessment

A structured medico-legal service for UHNW patients — conducted by Dr. Joseph Leong, Senior Consultant Psychiatrist

What this is

The Generational Legacy Assessment is a three-part structured medico-legal service for UHNW individuals who wish to ensure their legacy intentions are recorded with the rigour necessary to withstand scrutiny. It is not a full-fledged psychiatric assessment. Its purpose is to create a contemporaneous, professionally witnessed record of the patient's clarity of mind, intentions, and family context — produced at a specific point in time, signed by a Senior Consultant Psychiatrist, and suitable for reference in trust, estate, and capacity proceedings.

How to position this to patients

When the patient says: "I cannot change my family's mindset"

"You don't need to. What we're doing today isn't about changing anyone's mind. It's about empowering you to pass on your legacy by recording your wishes so clearly that your family members cannot challenge or influence it later. Your family's mindset becomes irrelevant when your statement is defensible."

The core message

This is not family therapy. This is a legacy protection measure. Frame it as an act of agency and “generative drive” - the desire to create something meaningful for the next generation.

Pricing justification

The fee is for a highly defensible and rigorous report that may determine the outcome of proceedings involving assets of significant value. UHNW patients spend multiples of this figure on estate planning and legal fees. The Generational Legacy Assessment belongs in the same category and not in the same conversation as a therapy session.

Referral pathway

The most effective referral channel is the patient's own lawyer — specifically private wealth and trust partners who are already advising on estate and succession matters. When a trusted partner says "I would strongly recommend Dr. Joseph's assessment before we finalise this," the patient does not need to be independently convinced. The June roundtable and the pre-meeting 1:1 conversations with partners are the primary mechanism for seeding this referral behaviour.

Clinical protocol — Dr. Joseph Leong, Promises Healthcare

Session 3 family mapping — protocol and consent framework

Governing document for the conduct of family mapping sessions. Covers pre-session conditions, consent requirements, in-session ground rules, and response protocols for adversarial or disruptive behaviour.

Phase 1

Pre-session — conditions for Session 3 to proceed

Step 1 — Patient initiates, not family

Session 3 may only be scheduled at the explicit request of the patient following Session 2. Family members may not request or initiate Session 3 independently. This preserves the patient's agency as the primary subject of the assessment and prevents family members from using the session to assert their own position.

Step 2 — Patient selects who attends

The patient alone determines which family members are invited to Session 3. Dr Joe does not accept requests from family members to include or exclude other individuals. The patient's selection is documented prior to the session and forms part of the assessment record.

Step 3 — Pre-session family briefing (without patient)

Before Session 3, Dr Joe or a Promises coordinator conducts a brief orientation with the invited family members — without the patient present. Family members who decline the briefing or express unwillingness to comply with ground rules are not admitted to Session 3.

  • Session is an observational documentation exercise, not therapy or mediation
  • Dr Joe's role is to observe and record, not to facilitate or adjudicate
  • All participants consent to session recording and documentation
  • Dr Joe retains the right to pause or terminate the session at any point

Step 4 — Dr Joe's right to decline or postpone

If Dr Joe determines that the family composition presents a material risk to the integrity of the documentation session — including known hostility between parties, legal proceedings already commenced, or evidence of prior undue influence — he may decline to proceed and document his reasons. Session 3 is offered at clinical discretion, not as an obligation.

Phase 2

Consent requirements — all participants

All participants must provide written informed consent before the session commences, covering the following:

Recording consent

Session will be audio or video recorded. Recording may be referenced in the formal clinical report.

Documentation consent

Dr Joe will take observational notes. Observations may be cited in the final signed report.

Purpose acknowledgement

Participant confirms this is a documentation session, not a therapeutic or mediation session.

Report use acknowledgement

Participant acknowledges the report may be referenced in legal proceedings relating to the patient's estate or capacity.

Ground rules consent

Participant agrees to session ground rules and acknowledges Dr Joe's right to pause or terminate.

Independent advice acknowledgement

Family members are advised they may wish to seek independent legal advice before participating.

Phase 3

In-session — ground rules and opening script

Opening script — read by Dr Joe at the start of every Session 3

Script

"Thank you all for being here. I am Dr. Joseph Leong, Senior Consultant Psychiatrist at Promises Healthcare. I am not here today as a therapist or mediator. My role is to observe and document. Everything said and observed in this session will be recorded and may form part of a formal clinical report. Each of you has already signed a consent form confirming your understanding of this. The session is structured around questions I will direct primarily to [patient's name]. Family members are welcome to respond when invited to do so. I ask that you allow [patient's name] to speak without interruption. If at any point I ask you to pause, please do so. I have the right to pause or end the session if I consider it necessary. Are there any questions before we begin?"

Ground rules in force throughout

  • Only one person speaks at a time
  • Family members do not answer questions directed at the patient
  • Family members do not interrupt or correct the patient's responses
  • No non-verbal communication directed at the patient in response to their answers
  • Dr Joe directs the structure and pace of the session at all times
  • Any participant may request a short break — but may not request a private consultation with the patient
Phase 4

In-session — response protocols for adversarial behaviour

Scenario A — Family member interrupts or speaks over the patient

Dr Joe addresses the family member directly: "I need to ask you to wait until I invite you to respond." If the interruption recurs, Dr Joe notes it for the record. The interruption itself becomes part of the documented observational record.

Scenario B — Family member becomes hostile or aggressive

Dr Joe pauses the session. He assesses whether the patient appears distressed or pressured. If yes, he invites the family member to step outside and continues with the patient alone, documenting the circumstances. If hostility continues, Dr Joe terminates the session. A partial session record is still a valid and usable document.

Scenario C — Patient appears distressed or changes prior position in family presence

Dr Joe does not redirect or attempt to resolve the pressure in the moment. He documents the observed change carefully and invites family members to step outside at an appropriate pause. The divergence between the patient's responses alone (Session 2) versus in family presence (Session 3) is itself significant clinical evidence.

Scenario D — Family member refuses or withdraws consent mid-session

Dr Joe acknowledges the withdrawal without debate and excuses the family member. The session continues with remaining participants. The patient's reaction to the withdrawal is part of the record.

Scenario E — Legal proceedings between parties disclosed during the session

Dr Joe pauses immediately and does not continue until he has obtained independent advice on whether proceeding creates a professional conflict. He documents the disclosure and suspends the session pending review.

Post-session documentation requirements

What Dr Joe documents after every Session 3

  • Names and relationships of all family members present
  • Any family member who declined to attend or withdrew consent
  • Seating arrangement and physical positioning in the room
  • Observable non-verbal dynamics — eye contact, body language, physical proximity
  • Any ground rule violations and Dr Joe's response
  • Any divergence between patient responses in Session 2 versus Session 3
  • Dr Joe's overall clinical observation of the patient's affect, composure, and consistency
  • Whether the session was completed, paused, or terminated — and the reason
Patient-facing briefing

What Dr Joe tells the patient before Session 3 is scheduled

Patient briefing script

"Session 3 is an optional part of the Generational Legacy Assessment. Its purpose is to create a documented record of your family context — observed directly by me — at this point in time. You decide who attends. Before the session, I will briefly meet with the family members you have invited to explain the format and obtain their consent. During the session, I will direct questions primarily to you. Family members will be invited to respond at specific points. I will be taking notes throughout and the session will be recorded. Everything observed becomes part of your assessment record. You may stop the session at any time. This session is not therapy. It is not mediation. It is a professionally witnessed record of your family context, produced at a time when your intentions and your clarity of mind are documented and verified."
